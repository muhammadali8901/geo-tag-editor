Add Location to Photos on Android — Pixel, Samsung & OnePlus (2026)

By Alex Rivers • Updated March 30, 2026 • 9 min read

Most "how to geotag on Android" guides describe an Android that no longer exists. This one is current as of Android 15 / One UI 7 / OxygenOS 14, and it splits the problem into the two questions Android users actually have: "how do I make new photos save the location?" and "how do I add location to a photo I already took?"

Two Different Problems, Two Different Fixes

Almost every question about geotagging on Android comes down to one of these two:

  • New photos aren't getting GPS. The fix is in the camera app's settings, not the system Settings — and it's not in the same place on every brand.
  • Old photos have no GPS and you want to add it. Android's gallery apps don't expose this, so you need either Google Photos' built-in editor or an external tool.

The two halves of this guide handle them in turn.

Part 1: Make New Photos Save the Location

The setting is called slightly different things on each brand. The system-wide Location toggle in Settings → Location must be on first; that's the prerequisite for everything below.

Pixel (Google Camera, Android 14 / 15)

  1. Open the Camera app.
  2. Tap the down arrow at the top of the viewfinder, then the gear icon for Settings.
  3. Tap More settings.
  4. Toggle Save location on. (If the toggle is greyed out, it's because the Camera app doesn't yet have location permission — see below.)

Permission step Pixel sometimes hides: the first time you toggle Save location on, Android will prompt for the Camera app's location permission. If you dismissed it, go to Settings → Apps → Camera → Permissions → Location → choose "Allow only while using the app" (Pixels do not need "Allow all the time" for this).

Samsung Galaxy (One UI 6 / 7, Android 14 / 15)

  1. Open Camera.
  2. Tap the gear icon in the top-left of the viewfinder.
  3. Scroll down and toggle Location tags on.
  4. Samsung shows a permission prompt the first time you do this — choose Allow.

One UI 7 also added a "Location accuracy" sub-setting under Settings → Location → Location services. If your Galaxy's photo locations are off by a kilometre, that's the toggle to turn on — it lets the phone use Wi-Fi and Bluetooth to refine the GPS fix.

OnePlus (OxygenOS 14)

  1. Open Camera.
  2. Tap the three-line menu in the top corner, then the gear icon.
  3. Toggle Geo location (sometimes labelled "Location") on.

Xiaomi / Redmi (HyperOS / MIUI 14)

  1. Open Camera.
  2. Tap the three-line menu → Settings (gear icon).
  3. Toggle Save location info on.

Why GPS still doesn't appear after enabling the toggle

The setting is not the only requirement. Three other things have to be true:

  1. Settings → Location (the system toggle) is on.
  2. The Camera app has the Location permission, set to "Allow only while using the app" or higher. Pixels and Samsungs both check this; if you tapped "Don't allow" earlier, the camera silently records no GPS.
  3. The phone has a current GPS fix at the moment you press the shutter. Indoors or in basements that can take 10–30 seconds. The first photo of a session is the most likely to be missing GPS for this reason.

Part 2: Add Location to Photos Already on Your Phone

This is where Android stops being uniform. The default gallery on most phones (Samsung Gallery, Pixel's Photos app, OnePlus Gallery) shows the GPS but doesn't let you edit it. Here are the methods that actually work in 2026.

Method 1: Google Photos (the simplest, works on every Android)

  1. Open the photo in Google Photos.
  2. Swipe up on the photo, or tap the three-dot menu → Details.
  3. Under the location section, tap "Add a location" (or "Edit" if it has one already).
  4. Search for a place, or drop a pin on the map.
  5. Tap Save.

Important caveat that catches a lot of people: by default Google Photos stores this in its own database, not in the photo file's EXIF. If you download the file, the GPS will still be empty. To make the location permanent in the file itself, also use one of the methods below — or just edit the file directly with a browser tool before re-uploading.

Method 2: An in-browser editor (writes to the file itself)

  1. Open Chrome on the phone and go to geotagseditor.online/add-gps-to-photo-online/.
  2. Tap the upload area and pick the photo from your gallery.
  3. Drag the marker on the map or paste in coordinates from Google Maps.
  4. Tap Apply, then Download. The new file lands in your Downloads folder with the GPS written into the EXIF.

Nothing leaves your phone — the editor processes the file in the browser tab. This is the path most Android users will find easiest because there's no app install and the change is permanent in the file.

Method 3: A dedicated EXIF-editor app from Play Store

If you find yourself doing this regularly, an app makes sense. Two long-running options worth knowing about:

  • Photo Exif Editor (by Banana Studio) — free with ads, opens any JPEG from the gallery, lets you set GPS by typing coordinates or picking a map point, writes the change back to the file.
  • EXIF Editor (by Banana Studio, separate app) — broader EXIF editing including timestamp and camera fields, GPS included.

Avoid apps that ask for "all files" permission but don't explain why, or that send the photo to a server for processing. Read the permissions screen before installing — Play Store reviews are not always reliable for this kind of thing.

Method 4: Geotagging from a GPX track (for travel)

If you logged a GPX track while you were out (apps like OsmAnd, Locus Map, GPSLogger, or Strava), you can match photos to your route by timestamp instead of tagging each one by hand. This is how serious travel photographers do it. The workflow is:

  1. Export the day's GPX from your tracking app.
  2. Either: copy the photos and the GPX to a desktop and run them through GeoSetter (Windows, free) or ExifTool (-geotag flag); or use an Android app like Geotag Photos Pro that accepts a GPX and applies it on-device.

The catch: your phone's clock and the GPX clock have to agree. If they're more than a few seconds off, photos snap to the wrong points on the route. Check both before relying on this method.

How to Find Coordinates Quickly on Android

You don't need a separate app for this. Google Maps already does it:

  1. Open Google Maps.
  2. Long-press the spot on the map. A red pin drops and a card slides up from the bottom.
  3. The card shows the latitude and longitude (e.g., 48.8584, 2.2945). Tap to copy.
  4. Paste into whichever editor you're using.

Verifying the GPS After You've Added It

Three quick checks:

  • Google Photos: open the photo, swipe up — the location section should now show a map and address.
  • Samsung Gallery: open the photo, tap the three-dot menu → Details → look for the GPS coordinates.
  • Re-upload to the browser editor: if a tool reads the GPS back, the EXIF write succeeded. This is the most reliable check — gallery apps sometimes cache and show a stale "no location" status for a few seconds.

Privacy: One Setting Worth Knowing About

Android 14 added a per-app permission distinction between "approximate location" and "precise location". Apps that don't strictly need your exact spot — most weather apps, news apps, social apps — should be set to Approximate in Settings → Apps → [app] → Permissions → Location. The Camera app needs Precise; almost nothing else does.
