How to Remove GPS Location Data From Photos (Complete Guide)

By Alex Rivers • Updated March 13, 2026 • 10 min read

Learn how to remove GPS location data from photos and delete geotag metadata easily. Step-by-step guide for mobile, desktop, and online tools.

Table of Contents

  • Introduction
  • What Is GPS Metadata?
  • Privacy Risks of GPS Photos
  • How to Check for Location Data

Key Takeaways

  • GPS coordinates are often embedded in photo EXIF metadata by default.
  • Removing geotags before sharing protects your privacy and reputation.
  • Online tools can remove location data quickly without altering image quality.
  • Review your photo metadata before you post images from home, work, or local events.

Introduction

Digital photos often carry more information than what you see on the screen. When a phone or camera records an image, it can also store hidden GPS coordinates and other metadata that reveal exactly where the shot was taken.

That is why many people now take an extra step before sharing pictures: they remove location data from the file. This is especially important when the photo was taken at a private address, a local event, or near a neighbourhood landmark.

What Is GPS Metadata?

GPS metadata is part of the EXIF data embedded in JPEG files. It usually contains:

  • Latitude and longitude
  • Altitude and direction
  • Date and time
  • Device make and model
  • Camera settings such as aperture and shutter speed

For example, a photo taken in a café on your city’s main street may include exact coordinates, the time it was taken, and the type of phone used.

Privacy Risks of GPS Photos

GPS data can expose details you did not intend to share. Common risks include:

1. Releasing your home or workplace location

Photos taken at or near private addresses can reveal your daily routines. When shared publicly, a photo from your living room or local café can give away the exact street and block.

2. Revealing sensitive travel details

Travel photos from hotels, airports, or quiet neighbourhoods may include location tags that show where you were at a specific moment. That information can be used to infer your travel dates and route.

3. Increasing the risk of theft or stalking

GPS tags can make it easier for bad actors to identify where you live or where your family spends time. For that reason, many privacy experts recommend removing geotags from shared images.

How to Check for Location Data

Before removing GPS metadata, it helps to verify whether it is present. You can do this in several ways:

  • On Windows, right-click the file, choose Properties, then open the Details tab.
  • On Mac, open the image in Preview and select Tools > Show Inspector.
  • On mobile, view image details in the gallery app or share the image with a metadata reader.

For a fast privacy check, upload the photo to a trusted metadata viewer and look for a GPS or Location section.

Removal Methods

There are several reliable methods to remove location data from photos. The safest options are those that keep your files on your own device or in your browser.

Method 1: Use an online privacy tool

A browser-based tool can remove GPS metadata without uploading the image to a remote server. This is the quickest way to clean a photo before posting it to social media, email, or a local blog.

The process is simple:

  1. Open the tool in your browser.
  2. Upload the JPEG photo.
  3. Choose to remove GPS/location metadata.
  4. Download the cleaned image.

Method 2: Remove metadata on Windows

Windows includes a built-in option to remove properties from a photo:

  1. Right-click the file and select Properties.
  2. Choose the Details tab.
  3. Click Remove Properties and Personal Information.
  4. Select the GPS-related fields and save a copy.

Method 3: Remove metadata on Mac

Mac users can remove location data using Preview:

  1. Open the image in Preview.
  2. Go to Tools > Show Inspector.
  3. Select the GPS tab.
  4. Click Remove Location Info and save the file.

Method 4: Turn off location services for the camera

Stopping new photos from recording GPS data is also important. On most phones, you can disable location access for the camera app so future pictures do not include geotags.

When to Keep Location Data

There are times when GPS metadata is useful. Keep it when you are using images for:

  • Personal photo organisation
  • Travel logs
  • GIS or mapping projects
  • Photography portfolios that need location context

For most public sharing, though, removing GPS location data is the safer choice.

Final Thoughts

Removing GPS location data from photos is a small step with a large privacy benefit. Whether you are posting a picture from a local market, a quiet park, or a private home, deleting the hidden coordinates helps keep your personal information secure.

Use a trusted tool to remove geotags before sharing images online, and make it part of your normal photo-sharing routine. This is one of the easiest ways to protect yourself, your family, and your business from accidental exposure.
